Data & methods

Discrete choice experiment and mandate attributes

The tool is powered by discrete choice experiment (DCE) data collected in Australia, Italy, and France. Each choice task presented two vaccine mandate policies and a “no mandate” option. Mandates were described using four attributes:

  • Scope of mandate: High-risk occupations only vs All occupations & public spaces.
  • Exemption policy: Medical only, Medical + religious, or Medical + religious + personal belief.
  • Coverage requirement to lift mandate: mandate lifted at 50%, 70%, or 90% of the population vaccinated.
  • Estimated lives saved: 10, 25, 35, or 40 lives saved per 100,000 people.

Outbreak scenarios

The DCE was fielded under two epidemiological frames. In the mild outbreak scenario, the virus was described as causing mostly flu-like illness, with fewer than 5 deaths per 100,000, slow growth in cases (~5% per week), and hospitals under pressure but coping. In the severe outbreak scenario, more than 15 deaths per 100,000, rapid growth (~20% per week), and hospitals near capacity were described, with mandates framed as urgent tools to save lives and protect health systems.

Preference models

For each country–scenario combination, mixed logit (MXL) models in preference space were estimated using panel data, with normally distributed random parameters for all attributes. The model captures unobserved heterogeneity in preferences for mandate scope, exemptions, coverage thresholds, and expected lives saved. Latent class (LC) logit models identified mandate-supporter and mandate-resister classes based on the alternative-specific constant for the “No mandate” option. Class membership was related to covariates including trust, vaccine attitudes, numeracy, political orientation, and demographics.

The tool combines the MXL and LC results into a composite uptake engine. Utilities are evaluated at the chosen mandate design, converted into choice probabilities using the logit formula, and aggregated across individuals or classes to obtain predicted uptake by country, scenario, and subgroup.

Epidemiological and economic modelling

Benefit modules implement static attack-rate calculations and SEIR-style transmission dynamics to translate increased vaccination uptake into cases, hospitalisations, and deaths averted, including long-COVID morbidity. QALYs and DALYs are computed from mortality and morbidity profiles, with optional equity weights and monetisation using country-specific values of a statistical life (VSL) or value per life-year. Cost modules draw on international guidance (e.g., OSHA, CMS, WHO costing manuals, CDC, Gavi, NACI) to represent public-sector, employer-side, and vaccination-programme costs. Deterministic and probabilistic sensitivity analysis modules allow users to explore the robustness of cost–effectiveness conclusions to uncertainty in key parameters.

This decision-aid is designed for scenario exploration and high-level economic evaluation. It does not replace detailed country-specific modelling, regulatory appraisal, or ethical deliberation. All parameter values and assumptions should be reviewed and adapted before use in formal policy processes.
